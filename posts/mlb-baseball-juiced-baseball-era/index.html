What MLB Players Benefited the Most From The Juiced Ball Era?

mlb
baseball
juicedbaseballs
Author

Jack Praeuner

Published

April 10, 2026

The resurgence of the “juiced ball” in Major League Baseball is one of the most fascinating periods in recent sports memory. From 2017 to 2019, it seemed as though baseballs were flying. Routine fly balls to the warning track turned into home runs that cleared the wall by 50 feet, bench players with no track record of power were suddenly flirting with 30-homer seasons, and a league-wide offensive explosion made even the most casual fans realize that the physics of the game had shifted.

So this begs the question: What players benefited the most from the juiced ball resurgence, and how much of an impact did the baseballs really have on production?

Let’s take a look at some numbers.

Code 
library(tidyverse)
library(ggalt)
library(ggplot2)
library(ggrepel)

twentynineteenbatting <- read_csv ("data/2019standardbatting.csv") |> mutate(Player = gsub("#", "", Player, fixed=TRUE))|> mutate(Player = gsub("*", "", Player, fixed=TRUE))

twentyseventeenbatting <- read_csv ("data/2017standardbatting.csv")|> mutate(Player = gsub("#", "", Player, fixed=TRUE))|> mutate(Player = gsub("*", "", Player, fixed=TRUE))

twentyeightteenbatting <- read_csv ("data/2018standardbatting.csv")|> mutate(Player = gsub("#", "", Player, fixed=TRUE))|> mutate(Player = gsub("*", "", Player, fixed=TRUE))

twentytwentythreebatting <- read_csv ("data/2023standardbatting.csv")|> mutate(Player = gsub("#", "", Player, fixed=TRUE))|> mutate(Player = gsub("*", "", Player, fixed=TRUE))

twentytwentyfourbatting <- read_csv ("data/2024standardbatting.csv")|> mutate(Player = gsub("#", "", Player, fixed=TRUE))|> mutate(Player = gsub("*", "", Player, fixed=TRUE))

twentytwentyfivebatting <- read_csv ("data/2025standardbatting.csv")|> mutate(Player = gsub("#", "", Player, fixed=TRUE))|> mutate(Player = gsub("*", "", Player, fixed=TRUE))

juiced_era_all_players <- bind_rows(
  twentyseventeenbatting, 
  twentyeightteenbatting, 
  twentynineteenbatting
) |> mutate(Era = "Juiced Ball (2017-19)")

modern_era_all_players <- bind_rows(
  twentytwentythreebatting,
  twentytwentyfourbatting,
  twentytwentyfivebatting
) |> mutate(Era = "Modern Era (2023-25)")

all_batting_data <- bind_rows(juiced_era_all_players, modern_era_all_players)

juiced_era_data <- bind_rows(
  twentyseventeenbatting |> mutate(Year = 2017),
  twentyeightteenbatting |> mutate(Year = 2018),
  twentynineteenbatting |> mutate(Year = 2019)
) |> 
  filter(!is.na(HR)) |> 
  mutate(Era = "Juiced Ball (2017-19)")

modern_era_data <- bind_rows(
  twentytwentythreebatting |> mutate(Year = 2023),
  twentytwentyfourbatting |> mutate(Year = 2024),
  twentytwentyfivebatting |> mutate(Year = 2025)
) |> 
  filter(!is.na(HR)) |> 
  mutate(Era = "Modern Era (2023-25)")

era_summary <- bind_rows(juiced_era_data, modern_era_data) |>
  filter(!is.na(HR)) |>
  group_by(Player, Age, Year, Era) |> 
  slice_max(order_by = nchar(as.character(Team)), n = 1, with_ties = FALSE) |>
  ungroup() |>
  group_by(Era) |>
  summarize(Total_HRs = sum(as.numeric(HR), na.rm = TRUE))

juiced_highlight <- era_summary |> 
  filter(Era == "Juiced Ball (2017-19)")

ggplot() + 
  geom_bar(
    data=era_summary, 
    aes(x=Era, weight=Total_HRs), 
    fill="skyblue"
  ) + 
  geom_bar(
    data=juiced_highlight, 
    aes(x=Era, weight=Total_HRs), 
    fill="orange"
  ) +
  geom_text(
    data=era_summary, 
    aes(x=Era, y=Total_HRs, label=Total_HRs), 
    vjust=2, 
    fontface="bold"
  ) +
  theme_minimal() +
  labs(
    x="MLB Era", 
    y="Total Home Runs Hit", 
    title="The Juiced Ball Era: Baseballs were Flying in the Late 2010s", 
    subtitle="The league-wide home run total from 2017-19 significantly outpaces the 2023-25 period.", 
    caption="Source: Baseball-Reference | By Jack Praeuner"
  ) +
  theme(
    plot.title = element_text(face = "bold", size = 15),
    plot.subtitle = element_text(size = 10, color = "grey20"),
    plot.caption = element_text(size = 9, margin = margin(t = 10)),
    panel.grid.major.x = element_blank()
  )

When comparing the league-wide totals between the two eras: the Juiced Ball Era (2017-19), and the Modern Era (2023-25), the drop-off is staggering. During the three-year peak of the juiced ball, a total of 18,466 home runs were hit. In the last three seasons, that number fell to 16,971, nearly 1,500 fewer home runs in the same time span. That isn’t just a slight variation or a few cold weeks in April; it is a fundamental shift in how many runs were being manufactured via the long ball. In addition, the juiced-ball era saw the season home run record (total across all players) broken in 2017, when 6,105 long balls were hit. Oh, and two years later, the record was broken again, at the apex of the era, when an ungodly 6,776 home runs were hit in the 2019 MLB season.

Overall, in the late 2010s, it felt like the floor for being considered a “power hitter” had been lowered because the ball seemed to be doing so much of the work. But what about all the individual players in the league? Where do the totals rank when we look at everyone who suited up?

When you break it down using a scatterplot, you can see the density of that power surge across the eras. While outliers like Aaron Judge and Shohei Ohtani have actually hit more homers in the modern era, the majority have not.

Code 
all_batting_cleaned <- bind_rows(
  twentyseventeenbatting |> mutate(Year = 2017),
  twentyeightteenbatting |> mutate(Year = 2018),
  twentynineteenbatting |> mutate(Year = 2019),
  twentytwentythreebatting |> mutate(Year = 2023),
  twentytwentyfourbatting |> mutate(Year = 2024),
  twentytwentyfivebatting |> mutate(Year = 2025)
) |>
  filter(!is.na(HR)) |>
  mutate(Era = ifelse(Year <= 2019, "Juiced Ball (2017-19)", "Modern Era (2023-25)")) |>
  group_by(Player, Age, Year, Era) |> 
  slice_max(order_by = nchar(as.character(Team)), n = 1, with_ties = FALSE) |>
  ungroup()

player_scatterplot_data <- all_batting_cleaned |>
  group_by(Player, Era) |>
  summarize(Total_HRs = sum(as.numeric(HR), na.rm = TRUE)) |>
  ungroup()

top10players <- player_scatterplot_data |>
  arrange(desc(Total_HRs)) |>
  head(10)

ggplot() +
  geom_point(
    data=player_scatterplot_data, 
    aes(x=Era, y=Total_HRs, color=Era), 
    alpha=0.3,
    position = position_jitter(width = 0.25, seed = 42)
  ) +
  geom_text_repel(
    data=top10players, 
    aes(x=Era, y=Total_HRs, label=Player),
    size = 3,
    fontface = "bold",
    force = 2,            
    max.overlaps = 30,
    box.padding = 0.3,
    segment.color = 'grey50'
  ) +
  scale_color_manual(values=c(
    "Juiced Ball (2017-19)" = "orange", 
    "Modern Era (2023-25)" = "skyblue"
  )) +
  scale_y_continuous(limits = c(0, 180)) + 
  theme_minimal() +
  labs(
    x="MLB Era", 
    y="Total Home Runs (3-Year Total)", 
    title="Juiced Ball vs Modern Era: What Players Benefitted the Most?", 
    subtitle="Individual 3-year totals show that 32 of the top 50 players hit more homers in the juiced ball era.", 
    caption="Source: Baseball-Reference | By Jack Praeuner"
  ) +
  theme(
    plot.title = element_text(face = "bold", size = 15), 
    plot.subtitle = element_text(size = 10, color = "grey20"),
    plot.caption = element_text(size = 9, margin = margin(t = 10)),
    legend.position = "none",
    panel.grid.major.x = element_blank(),
    axis.text.x = element_text(size = 11, face = "bold")
  )

Out of the top 50 hitters with the most home runs in a three-year span, 32 of them did it in the juiced ball era. So while some players will always get their numbers simply because they are that talented, the league-wide environment back then was lifting players who might typically average 15 homers a year, into the 25-30 range.

The most telling visual, however, is the comparison of guys who played in all three years in both eras.

If you look at a dumbbell chart of the top 15 home run hitters who were active for all seasons in both eras, the trend is almost universal: 13 of the top 15 hit more home runs back when the baseballs were flying. Mike Trout and Nolan Arenado, for example, saw massive gaps between their production in the late 2010s compared to the last three seasons. While age and injuries play a factor, the consistency of this drop-off suggests the ball just isn’t traveling the way it used to.

Code 
dumbbell_data <- player_scatterplot_data |>
  pivot_wider(names_from = Era, values_from = Total_HRs) |>
  rename(Juiced = `Juiced Ball (2017-19)`, Modern = `Modern Era (2023-25)`) |>
  filter(!is.na(Juiced) & !is.na(Modern)) |>
  filter(!Player %in% c("Nelson Cruz", "Charlie Blackmon", "Joey Gallo", "J.D. Martinez", "Mike Moustakas"), 
         Modern > 5) |> 
  arrange(desc(Juiced)) |> 
  head(15)

ggplot() + 
  geom_segment(
    data=dumbbell_data, 
    aes(y=reorder(Player, Juiced), yend=Player, x=Modern, xend=Juiced),
    color="grey80",
    linewidth = 1.2
  ) + 
  geom_point(
    data=dumbbell_data,
    aes(y=Player, x=Modern, color="Modern Era (2023-25)"),

    aes(y=Player, x=Juiced, color="Juiced Ball (2017-19)"),

Ultimately, the data suggests that the Modern Era is actually a return to a more traditional style of baseball. The 2017 to 2019 period was an outlier where home runs were essentially being handed out at a discount. While it’s still unknown the exact reason as to why baseballs were flying more during this time, the numbers certainly back it up. Now that the ball seems to have settled down, we are seeing a game where power has to be earned again, and the massive totals of the late 2010s look more like a temporary explosion than a permanent change to the sport.
