Why Did Aaron Rodgers Only Win 1 Super Bowl With the Packers?

nfl
aaronrodgers
playoffs
Author

Jack Praeuner

Published

April 23, 2026

Aaron Rodgers. Nowadays, he’s almost as well-known for his controversial viewpoints as he is for his play as a quarterback in the National Football League, but what about his time in Green Bay? The Rodgers era in Green Bay (2008-2022) will always be defined by unbelievable throws, late-game comebacks, and many beatdowns of the Chicago Bears; yet, for a player who is often regarded as the most talented player the quarterback position has ever seen, how does such an elite talent only have 1 Super Bowl?

Critics are usually quick to blame Rodgers for many of the Packers’ postseason failures during his tenure, but a deeper look at the numbers shows that the blame might be going to the wrong place. So did the Packers defense actually fail Rodgers, or was he the problem?

Let’s take a look at some of the numbers.

gb_history <- read_csv("data/gb_team_history.csv", skip = 1) |>
  filter(Year >= 2008 & Year <= 2022) |>
  mutate(Year = as.numeric(gsub("[*+]", "", Year))) |>
  mutate(Tm = gsub("[*+]", "", Tm))

rodgers_clean_scores <- rodgersplayoffs |> 
  separate(Result, into=c("Outcome", "Scores"), sep=" ") |>
  mutate(Scores = gsub("(OT)", "", Scores, fixed=TRUE)) |>
  separate(Scores, into=c("Packers_Pts", "Opp_Pts"), sep="-") |>
  mutate(Packers_Pts = as.numeric(Packers_Pts), Opp_Pts = as.numeric(Opp_Pts)) |>
  mutate(Score_Label = paste0(format(Date, "%Y"), " ", Opp, " (", Packers_Pts, "-", Opp_Pts, ")"))

ggplot() +

When you look at the scoring totals in playoff games Rodgers lost, you can see a pretty staggering trend. Out of his 10 playoff losses with the Packers, only one time did Rodgers score less than 20 points, when he scored a measly 10 points against the 49ers in his final playoff game as a Packer in 2022.

However, even when you exclude this outlier, in his nine other playoff losses, he scored above the league average only three times: 45 against Arizona in 2010, 31 against San Francisco in 2013, and 26 against Tampa Bay in 2021. For context, the scoring average per team between 2008 and 2022 was 22.6 points. In six losses, Rodgers’ offense hovered right around this mark, scoring 20 points four times, and 21 or 22 points once. This is where the debate really begins.

While there are certainly losses where the defense is to blame, such as the Arizona shootout in 2010, where the defense gave up 45 (Rodgers got blind-sided strip-sacked for a touchdown to lose in overtime). MVP Matt Ryan and the Falcons scoring 44 in the NFC Championship in 2017. Or even when the Packers gave up 45 to Colin Kaepernick in 2013. Even if Rodgers had played perfectly in all those games, the Packers still likely would have lost, given how bad the defense played. But what about games like Seattle in 2015, Arizona in 2016, and San Francisco in 2014? In both the Seattle and Arizona games, Rodgers’ team lost the coin toss in overtime and never saw the ball again. The San Francisco loss? Well, there’s no excuse for that, as Rodgers went three and out on his final possession and Phil Dawson walked the game off as time expired to win 23-20.

That’s enough talk about the games where the Packers’ defense may not be the ones to blame; let’s take a look at the league rankings in terms of points per game for both the offense and defense of the Packers in the Rodgers era, to see who we should really be pointing the finger at.

When looking at the line chart, you can see the Packers offense, represented by the teal line, is consistently in the top 10 range of points per game in the NFL, with a few slight dips, most notably in 2017, when Rodgers missed half the season due to a broken collarbone, and then rested once the Packers were eliminated from playoff contention.

On the other hand, in 14 fully healthy seasons as the Packers starter, the Packers defense was ranked ahead of the offense only three times in terms of points per game ranking in the league. Furthermore, the one season where Rodgers had a top 5 defense, 2010, he won the Super Bowl. To go one step further, although the chart shows each unit’s ranking in terms of points per game, in addition to being the 19th-ranked defense in points per game in 2011, that same group was 32nd in yards allowed, dead last. The team’s record that season: 15-1. So, while there are a few seasons where the defense was better than the offense, for the most part, like many great quarterbacks, the offense was vastly superior to the defense.

But what about his individual performances in all his playoff games? We’ve already dug into why the defense might have been the problem in the games he lost, as there were a handful of games where they gave up more than 30, but what about Rodgers? Should his play in some of these losses have yielded more favorable results? On the other hand, should his play in some of his wins have resulted in more losses?

To see the actual efficiency compared to the final results, we can look at Rodgers’ individual game logs from these postseason runs.

This summary table highlights how efficient Rodgers actually was during all of his playoff games. For context, the league average passer rating is around 91.3, and when you look at his passer ratings, in almost all of his wins, his passer rating was above-average to elite. The only win Rodgers has where his passer rating was not at least above league average was the 2011 NFC Championship game against Chicago, when he put up a lackluster 55.8 rating.

Even in some of his losses, such as a 121.4 rating against Arizona in 2010, 101.6 against Tampa in 2021, and a 97.8 against San Francisco in 2014, Rodgers play graded out to be good enough to win these games. However, outside of the one outlier win against Chicago, if Rodgers passer rating was ever right around league average or below, his team never won a single playoff game. This table shows that in order for the Packers to win, Rodgers had to play at least above-average, and often elite, for the Packers to even have a chance of winning, and sometimes, even that wasn’t enough.

Of course, like any quarterback, there are games Rodgers lost that he certainly did not deserve to win. In 2012, a year after winning the Super Bowl, even though the Packers’ defense gave up 37 points, Rodgers finished with an underwhelming 78.5 rating. While a Brandon Bostic muff on an onside kick in the 2015 NFC Championship against Seattle with two minutes to go is an all-time unlucky break, Rodgers 55.8 rating for that game was certainly nothing to praise. Other games, such as the divisional round game against Arizona in 2016, where Rodgers made arguably two of the top ten greatest throws in NFL history on the final drive to force overtime, still saw him finish with a less-than-stellar 77.9 rating.

While there were plenty of games where the defense let him down, Rodgers is not void of blame. While he certainly had more unfortunate breaks compared to other all-time great quarterbacks, even if his defense had played better, there are still multiple losses where his play didn’t dictate winning.

In conclusion, is it fair to say that if Rodgers had more defensive help, he would have won more? I think the data shows that while it would be a fair claim to make, it isn’t as if he would have 10+ rings or something crazy like that. More realistically, had Rodgers gotten more help from his defense, in combination with more favorable luck, it’s fair to assume that the Packers could have won at least 2-3 more Super Bowls during his time with the team. Remember, the one time he had a top 5 defense, he won the Super Bowl.
